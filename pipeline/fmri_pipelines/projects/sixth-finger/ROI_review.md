# Neural substrates of individual finger representation in fMRI

## Section 1: Finger motor execution

**Primary motor cortex (M1) contains extensively overlapping yet distinguishable finger representations.** Classical fMRI mapping reveals that individual finger activations share substantial cortical territory, with only a gross somatotopic gradient — thumb positioned ventrolaterally, little finger dorsomedially — discernible from center-of-mass coordinates averaged across subjects (Dechent & Frahm, 2003, *Human Brain Mapping*). Hlustík et al. (2001, *Cerebral Cortex*) confirmed orderly somatotopy in M1 and S1 for individual digits, with M1 showing greater inter-finger overlap than S1. Critically, multivariate pattern analysis (MVPA) has transformed this picture: Ejaz, Hamada, and Diedrichsen (2015, *Nature Neuroscience*) demonstrated that while spatial layouts of finger patterns vary across individuals, the **representational geometry** — the relative similarity structure between fingers — is invariant and better explained by natural hand-use statistics than by muscle anatomy. The thumb exhibits the most distinctive pattern. Arbuckle et al. (2020, *Journal of Neuroscience*) further showed that M1 fMRI patterns are distinct across fingers but similar for flexion versus extension of the same finger, indicating that fMRI signals in M1 reflect finger identity rather than movement direction.

**Premotor cortex (PMd and PMv) contributes to finger selection and sequence encoding.** PMd and PMv show weaker single-finger representations than M1 but critically encode multi-finger sequences in ways that cannot be explained by linear combinations of individual finger patterns (Yokoi, Arbuckle, & Diedrichsen, 2018, *Journal of Neuroscience*). Kornysheva and Diedrichsen (2014, *eLife*) found that premotor areas independently represent spatial (finger order) and temporal (rhythm) features of trained sequences, with a dorsal-spatial to ventral-temporal gradient. PMd also maintains sequence-specific representations during movement preparation (Ariani, Shahbazi, & Diedrichsen, 2025, *Journal of Neuroscience*).

**SMA proper is preferentially activated during sequential finger movements** compared to simple repetitive actions, while pre-SMA is more engaged during self-initiated movements and movement planning (Deiber et al., 1999, *Journal of Neurophysiology*). Training strengthens sequence-specific representations in SMA (Wiestler & Diedrichsen, 2013, *eLife*). The **anterior superior parietal lobule (SPLa)** encodes hierarchical sequence structure — chunks and transitions — that M1 does not represent, forming a frontoparietal planning network with PMd (Yokoi & Diedrichsen, 2019, *Neuron*).

**Subcortically**, the cerebellum contains two somatomotor representations in lobules IV–V and VIII with individual finger-specific patches smaller than 3 mm, detectable only via MVPA (Wiestler, McGonigle, & Diedrichsen, 2011, *Journal of Neurophysiology*). In the **basal ganglia**, motor sequence learning drives a characteristic shift from rostrodorsal associative to caudoventral sensorimotor territories of the putamen (Lehéricy et al., 2005, *PNAS*), and distributed striatal patterns encode specific finger sequences across motor, associative, and limbic territories simultaneously (Beukema, Simonyan, & Bhatt, 2020, *Scientific Reports*). The **thalamus** shows somatotopic finger-movement responses in ventrolateral nuclei (VPL, VLa, VLp), relaying information from basal ganglia and cerebellar circuits to cortex.

## Section 2: Finger motor imagery

**Motor imagery of finger movements recruits a premotor–parietal network with weaker, debated M1 involvement.** Two large meta-analyses — Hétu et al. (2013, *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*; 75 studies) and Hardwick et al. (2018, *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*; 303 experiments) — converge on a core imagery network comprising **SMA, premotor cortex (dorsal and ventral), superior and inferior parietal lobules, putamen, and cerebellum**, with M1 not consistently activated at the meta-analytic level. However, individual finger-level studies tell a more nuanced story. Porro et al. (1996, *Journal of Neuroscience*) found M1 activation during finger-to-thumb opposition imagery at approximately **30% of execution signal intensity**. Ehrsson, Geyer, and Naito (2003, *Journal of Neurophysiology*) demonstrated that finger imagery activates **somatotopically appropriate** M1 sectors paralleling execution, though at reduced magnitude. This inconsistency may be explained by Kasess et al. (2008, *NeuroImage*), who used dynamic causal modeling to show that **SMA exerts active inhibitory influence on M1 during imagery**, gating motor output to prevent overt movement.

**Premotor cortex and SMA are the most reliably activated regions during finger imagery.** Lotze et al. (1999, *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*) found SMA and PMC consistently activated across all subjects during finger-to-thumb opposition imagery (with EMG control), and Hanakawa, Dimyan, and Hallett (2008, *Cerebral Cortex*) proposed a functional gradient from planning-oriented areas (pre-SMA) to output-oriented areas (M1), with imagery engaging the planning end. **Parietal cortex may be more active during imagery than execution**: Gerardin et al. (2000, *Cerebral Cortex*) found that imagery recruited superior and inferior parietal lobules more strongly than execution, consistent with parietal involvement in internal motor simulation. Pilgramm et al. (2016, *Human Brain Mapping*) confirmed that MVPA can decode the specific content of imagined hand actions from SPL, IPL, and IPS activity patterns.

**Subcortical involvement differs between imagery and execution.** The cerebellum is activated during finger imagery but at a more posterior/lateral focus than during execution (lobule HVII versus anterior lobe; Lotze et al., 1999). The putamen is consistently part of the imagery network, while the **caudate nuclei show greater activation during imagery than execution** (Gerardin et al., 2000). Kinesthetic imagery specifically drives stronger subcortical and motor-system engagement than visual imagery (Guillot et al., 2009, *Human Brain Mapping*; Solodkin et al., 2004, *Cerebral Cortex*).

**Key distinction for block-design interpretation:** imagery is not simply a weaker version of execution. The SMA→M1 connection is facilitatory during execution but **inhibitory during imagery** (Kasess et al., 2008; Solodkin et al., 2004). Imagery additionally recruits prefrontal cortex and caudate more than execution. For finger-specific decoding, MVPA approaches are essential — Heimhofer et al. (2024, *bioRxiv* preprint) demonstrated that imagery and execution activate similar somatotopic finger patterns in S1/M1 decodable by MVPA, stable across sessions two weeks apart, though univariate maps often lack finger selectivity during imagery.

## Section 3: Supernumerary digit representation

**A small but rapidly growing literature directly addresses how the brain represents extra fingers.** Mehring et al. (2019, *Nature Communications*) used **7T fMRI** in two individuals with congenital polydactyly (six fingers per hand) and found a **dedicated, distinct cortical representation** of the supernumerary finger in sensorimotor cortex — separate from all five typical fingers — with no deficit to existing finger representations. Mastria et al. (2023, *Journal of Neuroscience*) extended this finding using RSA, showing that even in polydactyly, finger representations are best explained by perceived hand structure.

For robotic augmentation, Kieliba, Clode, Maimon-Mor, and Makin (2021, *Science Robotics*) conducted the landmark study of the Third Thumb — a robotic supernumerary thumb controlled by toe pressure sensors. After five days of use, fMRI revealed that **individual biological finger representations in sensorimotor cortex became less distinct** (reduced multivariate dissimilarity), suggesting a trade-off between augmented and biological hand representation. These changes appeared reversible within one week. Complementing this, Rossi et al. (2021, *Scientific Reports*) used TMS to show that minutes of training with a robotic sixth finger rapidly generated novel "bioartificial" corticospinal motor synergies. Longitudinal BCI-controlled supernumerary finger training (4 weeks) modulates **cerebellar resting-state activity** and strengthens sensorimotor–prefrontal functional connectivity (Liu et al., 2022, *Frontiers in Neuroscience*; Huang et al., 2024, *npj Science of Learning*). Together, these studies suggest the motor system can rapidly incorporate an extra digit but may temporarily compromise biological finger distinctiveness — a critical consideration for interpreting imagery-training paradigms targeting a supernumerary thumb.

## Conclusion

Three insights are most relevant for interpreting fMRI data from a finger imagery/execution block design targeting a supernumerary thumb. First, **finger representations in M1 are overlapping but reliably decodable via MVPA**, with representational geometry shaped by use statistics rather than fixed anatomy — meaning training-induced reorganization is expected and detectable. Second, **imagery activates a premotor–parietal–subcortical network that partially overlaps execution**, with M1 involvement that is weak, somatotopically organized, and actively suppressed by SMA; MVPA is essential for detecting finger-specific imagery patterns. Third, **robotic augmentation drives measurable cortical plasticity** in finger representations within days, though existing evidence (Kieliba et al., 2021) suggests a possible trade-off with biological hand distinctiveness that warrants careful monitoring in longitudinal designs.
